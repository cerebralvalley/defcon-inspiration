The hardware hacking village grew out of community concerns over fundamental issues. We offer three of them for consideration, the doctrine of ownership, general confusion about the power of computation, and a vision of a more flexible hardware future. A founding principle of the doctrine of ownership is the right to enjoy. Owners should be able to alter the function of their purchase devices, repair and modify their hardware to maintain and enhance the value it produces, and select any service provider to repair and modify their hardware, even if that does not fit the original manufacturer's intent. Unfortunately, with electronics, these rights are being degraded. For example, if you owned your car with the same terms, you may soon own Nintendo's latest device, a Switch 2. Using the vehicle or device in a way that Nintendo does not intend or approve of means Nintendo could remotely turn your vehicle off and prevent it from ever starting again. This is commonly called bricking. Nintendo is not alone. Bankrupt companies and companies that have simply moved on to other products have left trails of bricked devices in their wake. The fundamental rights associated with ownership are being trampled and the contagion is spreading as electronics become key to more and more. The processors we make are general computing devices. Manufacturers create a device and software to make it do what they think will be of value. But the devices potential isn't limited to just that. Most people are confused about the possibilities of computational components, believing the devices capacities are far more limited than they are. In addition, people underestimate their own ability to modify devices to get much more out of those devices and the devices around them. With computers, the limits you have are memory and time. With those two limitations in mind, you can put a man on the moon with less computation power than many internet of things devices today. Yet, most people see devices as disposable rather than as starting points for their creative expression. This lack of vision has led to mountains of electronic waste, also known as e-waste. It has prevented whole industries from being born. The iPhone was revolutionary because it allowed end users to dream up new uses for existing hardware. A $400 billion industry was born. Imagine if instead of upgrading your phone every year or two, a local shop could upgrade your phone to the latest communication standard or add more memory or switch to a faster processor. This happens in Shenzhen, China. Why not here? For all these reasons and more, we teach the skills and provide a space to make your own use. You can decide to e-waste a device or give it a new purpose. You become the one that controls the hardware to the level you want to. The power is yours.
